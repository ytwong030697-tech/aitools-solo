The Core Stack: Tools I Actually Keep Around

AI Assistant Claude

Claude replaced ChatGPT for me about 8 months ago and I haven't gone back. The writing is sharper, it hallucinates less, and the Projects feature lets me keep separate workspaces for different clients without context bleeding. I use it for drafting blog posts, summarizing research papers, debugging code, and as a thinking partner when I'm stuck on strategy. The $20/month Pro plan is the first subscription I'd re-buy if I had to start over.

$20/mo Pro · Free tier available

Verdict: Core tool. Would re-buy immediately.

AI Search Perplexity

Google search results have gotten noticeably worse over the past two years. Half the first page is ads, the other half is SEO-optimized fluff that doesn't answer the question. Perplexity gives me cited answers in seconds. When I'm researching a new topic for an article, I start with Perplexity, grab the key sources, then go deep on the ones that matter. It's cut my research time by at least 40%.

$20/mo Pro · Free tier available

Verdict: Essential for research-heavy work.

Design Canva

I'm not a designer. I've never opened Photoshop. But I need blog headers, social media graphics, and the occasional pitch deck. Canva with its AI tools (Magic Write, Background Remover, AI image generator) covers pretty much everything I need. The brand kit feature keeps my colors and fonts consistent across everything I publish. The AI photo editor in the Pro plan has saved me from hiring a freelancer for quick touch-ups more times than I can count.

$13/mo Pro · Free tier available

Verdict: Worth it if you create visual content regularly.

Automation Make

I tried Zapier first. Then n8n. Both are fine. But Make is the one that stuck. The visual scenario builder just clicks for me in a way the others didn't. I've got about 12 scenarios running right now: auto-posting new blog articles to social media, forwarding certain emails to my task manager, generating weekly analytics summaries. The drag-and-drop interface means I can build a new workflow in 10 minutes without touching code.

$9/mo Core · Free tier available

Verdict: The best balance of power and ease of use.

Voice & Audio ElevenLabs

I started using ElevenLabs for a podcast pilot and ended up keeping it for voiceovers on YouTube shorts and client video projects. The voice cloning is unnervingly good — I generated a custom voice that sounds close enough to mine that listeners can't tell the difference. The text-to-speech API also feeds into some of my Make automations for generating audio versions of articles. Not a tool I'd recommend to everyone, but if audio content is part of your business, it's genuinely hard to beat.

$5/mo Starter · Free tier available

Verdict: Best-in-class for AI voice. Keep it if you do audio.

Newsletter Beehiiv

I moved my newsletter from Substack to Beehiiv six months ago. The main reason: Beehiiv is built for growth in a way Substack just isn't. The referral program, the recommendation network, the A/B testing on subject lines, the built-in ad network — all of it works together. My list has grown 3x since switching, and a chunk of that is from Beehiiv's cross-promotion features. The AI writing assistant inside the editor is fine, not amazing, but the platform as a whole is the best I've found for newsletter operators who actually want to scale.

Free up to 2,500 subs · $39/mo Scale plan

Verdict: The clear choice for newsletter-first creators.

Productivity Notion

Notion is my second brain. Client projects, content calendars, meeting notes, CRM, habit tracking — it all lives here. The AI features (Notion AI) are useful but not revolutionary. What keeps me paying is the database flexibility. I can build a lightweight CRM, a content pipeline, and a task tracker all in the same workspace, linked together. For a solopreneur who doesn't want to juggle five different tools, Notion replaces most of them.

$10/mo Plus · Free tier available

Verdict: The hub everything else connects to.

Hosting Hostinger

I host my portfolio site and two client projects on Hostinger. The AI website builder is surprisingly decent for landing pages — I built a service page in about 20 minutes that would have taken me two hours from scratch. The AI tools (heatmap predictions, SEO suggestions, content generator) are included even on the basic plan, which is rare in hosting. Uptime has been solid. Support responds in under 5 minutes when I need them.

$2.99/mo Premium · 48-month plan

Verdict: Best value hosting with genuinely useful AI features.

Lead Gen Apollo

Apollo is the tool I hesitated to include because it's more B2B sales than general solopreneur. But if you do any kind of client outreach — cold email, LinkedIn, finding decision-makers — Apollo's database of 275 million contacts plus AI-powered sequencing is hard to beat. I use it to find and reach out to potential clients for my consulting work. The AI email writer generates decent first drafts that I then rewrite. It's not cheap, but one new client pays for the annual subscription.

$49/mo Basic · Free tier available

Verdict: Expensive but pays for itself quickly. Sales-focused.

What I Cancelled (And Why Nobody Talks About It)

Not every tool earns its subscription. Here are two popular AI tools I cancelled in the last six months:

Jasper AI ($49/mo). Jasper was great in 2023 when it was one of the only AI writing tools. Now Claude and ChatGPT have caught up and surpassed it for most use cases. Jasper's templates are nice, but I don't need a separate tool just for marketing copy when Claude handles it just fine. The price-to-value ratio doesn't work anymore.

Copy.ai ($36/mo). Similar problem. Solid tool, but it solves a problem that no longer exists. When the general-purpose AI assistants got good enough at writing, the specialized writing tools became redundant for me. I know some marketing teams still swear by Copy.ai's workflows, but as a solo operator, I can't justify it.

The pattern is clear: specialized AI tools that do one thing well are getting squeezed by general-purpose assistants that do everything well enough. If a tool only does AI writing or AI image generation, it needs to be dramatically better than Claude or ChatGPT to survive. Most aren't.

How to Think About Your AI Stack

Don't try to build the "perfect" stack on day one. Start with one tool. Get comfortable with it. Add the next one when you have a specific problem it solves.

My stack evolved over 18 months. I didn't wake up one morning and subscribe to nine tools. I started with Claude, added Notion a month later, then Canva, then Make. Each addition happened because I hit a wall that a tool could solve.

Also worth mentioning: the total monthly cost of the tools I keep is around $155/month. That sounds like a lot until you compare it to hiring one part-time VA for $500/month. These tools handle the work of at least two assistants. The ROI math works if you're actually shipping work.

One more thing. The tools that survived in my stack share a common trait: they're general enough to use across multiple parts of my business. Claude writes blog posts, debugs code, and brainstorms strategy. Notion manages projects, stores knowledge, and tracks clients. Canva handles social graphics, pitch decks, and quick image edits. The tools I cancelled were all single-purpose.

The Honest Summary

If I had to rebuild from zero with a $50/month budget, I'd subscribe to Claude ($20), Canva ($13), and Make ($9). That covers writing, design, and automation. Everything else is optimization.

If you're a solopreneur trying to figure out which AI tools are worth paying for, start there. Three tools. Master them. Then add the rest when you have a clear need, not when a YouTube video tells you it's the "must-have tool of 2026."
