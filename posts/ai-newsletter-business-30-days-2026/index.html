I Built a Newsletter Business Using Only AI Tools for 30 Days — Here's What Actually Worked

May 31, 2026 · ~1,250 words · 8 tools tested · AI Tools Newsletter Solopreneur Case Study

Three weeks ago someone on Reddit asked a question that's been stuck in my head: "If you had $200 and zero skills, could you build a business with only AI tools?"

I had to find out.

So I did something incredibly stupid: I launched a newsletter. From scratch. No freelance writers, no designer, no editor, no social media manager. Just me, eight AI tools, and a very real fear that I'd spend 30 days producing content nobody would read.

Here's what happened — the tools, the costs, the subscriber numbers, and the stuff I wish someone had told me before I started.

The Rules I Set for Myself

I gave myself strict constraints to make this a genuine test:

  1. Every piece of writing had to pass through an AI tool at some point
  2. No hiring humans for editing, design, or research
  3. Budget cap: $200 total for tools and infrastructure
  4. Target: Publish 8 issues and hit 100 subscribers
  5. Topic: AI tools for non-technical people (meta, I know)

I picked this topic because it's broad enough to sustain a newsletter but specific enough that most readers share a clear problem: they feel left behind by AI and want practical, non-hype guidance.

The Stack: What I Used and Why

1. Beehiiv — Newsletter Platform

Where the newsletter actually lives. I chose Beehiiv over Substack for one reason: the growth tools. Built-in referral program, recommendation network, boosts, and ad network (if you get big enough). Substack feels like a blog with email. Beehiiv feels like a growth engine that also sends emails. The free plan covers you up to 2,500 subscribers, which is plenty when you're starting.

$0 (free plan) → Try Beehiiv Free

2. Claude (Anthropic) — Writing & Editing

This was my primary writer. Not in a "press button, get article" way — that produces garbage and everyone knows it. I used Claude as a thinking partner. I'd write a rough draft, feed it to Claude with the instruction "rip this apart and tell me what's missing," then rewrite based on what it found. The result was writing that still sounded like me, but was tighter and more thorough than what I'd produce alone. Claude's Projects feature let me upload my previous issues as context, so the tone stayed consistent across all 8 issues.

$20/month (Pro plan) → Try Claude Pro

3. Perplexity — Deep Research

Google has become unusable for research. Half the results are SEO spam, the other half are AI-generated articles citing each other in an ouroboros of nonsense. Perplexity cuts through that. I'd ask it "what are the top 5 AI tools launched this month that nobody's talking about yet" and get actual sources I could verify. Each issue included a "Tool of the Week" section that came entirely from Perplexity deep dives. The Pro Search feature was worth the $20 by itself.

$20/month (Pro) → Try Perplexity Pro

4. Canva — Newsletter Graphics & Social Media

I can't design. Never could. But every newsletter issue needed a header image, Twitter/X cards, and Instagram story graphics. Canva's AI features (Magic Design, background remover, text-to-image) meant I could produce 5-6 graphics per issue in under 15 minutes. Not award-winning design, but good enough that people shared them. The fact that it took 15 minutes instead of the 2 hours I'd spend fumbling in Photoshop made it a no-brainer.

$13/month (Pro) → Try Canva Pro

5. Make.com — Automation Glue

This is the thing that made the whole operation sustainable. I built three automations: (1) RSS feed monitoring for AI news → Claude summary → draft email, (2) new subscriber → personalized welcome email sequence, (3) social media cross-posting when each issue goes live. Without Make, I'd be spending 5-6 extra hours per week on manual busywork. The visual canvas is intuitive enough that I built all three automations in a single afternoon.

$9/month (Core plan) → Try Make.com Free

6. ElevenLabs — Audio Version

Starting in week 3, I added an audio version of each issue. ElevenLabs text-to-speech turned my 1,000-word newsletter into a 6-minute listen in under two minutes. Quality is genuinely unsettling — multiple subscribers asked who my "voice actor" was. The audio versions consistently got more engagement than the text-only ones. Not essential for starting out, but a great differentiator once you hit 10+ issues.

I wanted a simple landing page to collect emails outside of the Beehiiv ecosystem. Hostinger's AI website builder spat out a decent one-page site in about 20 minutes. It's not revolutionary, but $3/month for hosting plus a free domain for the first year is hard to argue with. What I Got Wrong

Several things didn't work at all:

  • AI-generated entire articles are obvious. I tried having Claude write a full issue once. It read like a Wikipedia article written by someone who's never felt an emotion. The unsubscribe rate on that issue was triple the average. Never again.
  • Social media distribution needs a human touch. Make.com could cross-post, but engagement was terrible until I started writing the social captions myself. People can smell automation from a mile away.
  • Growth is the hard part. The tools made production easy — dangerously easy. I could produce an issue in 90 minutes. Finding readers took 10x longer. Cross-promotions and Beehiiv's recommendation network were the only channels that moved the needle.
  • I should have started with a lead magnet. A free PDF, checklist, or tool comparison table would have doubled my conversion rate. I added one in week 4 and immediately saw a signup spike. Don't make this mistake — have something to give away before you publish issue #1.

Bottom Line: Would I Do It Again?

Yes, but differently. The AI tools are good enough to replace freelancers for production tasks. They're not remotely good enough to replace humans for things requiring judgment, taste, or genuine connection with readers.

The stack works. But the stack is the easy part — it took me one weekend to set up. Finding an audience? That still takes the one thing AI can't fake: showing up consistently and giving a damn about what you're making.

If You Want to Try This Yourself

Here's the exact setup I'd recommend, starting from zero:

  1. Pick a topic you actually care about. If you're not interested, your readers won't be either. AI can help with everything except faking enthusiasm.
  2. Start with Beehiiv's free plan. No reason to pay until you hit 2,500 subscribers. Sign up here.
  3. Use Claude for editing, not writing. Write your own drafts. Feed them to Claude for critique and restructuring. Your voice stays intact but the quality jumps significantly.
  4. Set up Make.com to handle the boring stuff. Welcome emails, social cross-posting, RSS monitoring. Start with the free tier here.
  5. Add audio with ElevenLabs once you have 10+ issues. It doubles as a podcast feed and catches people who prefer listening over reading.
  6. Don't obsess over tools. The tools matter way less than publishing consistently for 6 months. I'd rather have a mediocre stack and publish 50 issues than a perfect stack and publish 5.

I'm publishing this because I genuinely think more people should try building things with these tools. Not because the tools are magic — they're not — but because they lower the barrier from "I need $5,000 and a team" to "I need $70 and a Saturday afternoon." That's worth paying attention to.
