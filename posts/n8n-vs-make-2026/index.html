I spent April 2026 building the same five automations in both n8n and Make.com. A customer onboarding flow. An AI-powered Slack digest. A LinkedIn post scheduler. A lead enrichment pipeline. And a cross-platform invoice generator.

The short version: they're both genuinely good tools that solve fundamentally different problems. But the way they bill you, and what happens when your workflows actually run at scale, tells a story their marketing pages won't.

Here's what I found.

The 30-second summary

Go with Make if you want to connect mainstream SaaS tools right now, without touching a server or writing code. The visual builder is genuinely intuitive, and your marketing or ops team can be autonomous by end of day.

Go with n8n if you need data to stay on your own servers, want AI agents that actually make decisions instead of just calling APIs, or you're doing enough volume that per-operation pricing would eat your budget alive.

What they actually are

Both tools let you connect apps and build automated workflows. But their DNA is completely different.

Make (formerly Integromat, rebranded in 2022) is a cloud-only SaaS platform. You log in, drag modules onto a canvas, connect them, and hit run. Everything lives on their servers. It launched in 2012 and has had over a decade to build out its connector library — they claim 3,000+ native app integrations now.

n8n (pronounced "n-eight-n") is open-source. You can run it on your own machine with a single Docker command, or pay them to host it. It's younger — started in 2019 — but the AI-native features they've shipped in 2025-2026 have put them ahead of Make in one very specific area: autonomous AI agents that chain reasoning steps together.

I keep thinking about this framing: Make is the fastest path from zero to working automation. n8n is what you graduate to when you need control.

Here's the part that tripped me up: a "10-step workflow running 1,000 times" costs 10,000 operations on Make but 1,000 executions on n8n. If you're building multi-step automations — and you probably are — Make's pricing can surprise you at the end of the month.

To put real numbers on it: running 50,000 operations per month at an average of 8 steps per workflow costs about $10-15/month on a self-hosted n8n instance (you just pay the VPS), versus $48-96/month on Make's Pro plan with operation add-ons.

AI agent capabilities: n8n pulls ahead

This is the biggest differentiator in 2026. Both tools let you connect to OpenAI, Claude, and other LLMs. But n8n has native AI Agent nodes that do actual tool-use — the agent picks which step to run next based on the context, rather than just following a pre-defined path.

Make's AI features are still mostly "call this API and use the output in the next step." Their AI Agents app (launched early 2026) is a step forward, but it doesn't have the LangChain-level flexibility n8n offers. Several users on Reddit and automation forums have said they moved from Make to n8n specifically because Make's AI agent "cannot step outside the scenario it lives in."

Kyle Spitzley at HatchWorks wrote a good deep-dive on this: n8n's AI workflows can route decisions through an LLM, call external tools based on the response, and maintain memory across runs. Think: "analyze this support ticket, decide whether to escalate, draft a response if simple, or create a Jira ticket if complex." Make can do pieces of this, but n8n makes the decision logic feel more like an actual agent and less like a flowchart.

I built both versions of an AI support triage bot. The n8n version took longer to set up (about 3 hours vs 1.5 for Make), but the n8n one handles edge cases without me having to pre-define every possible branch. The Make version works great within its designed parameters and fails silently on anything unexpected. Which one is "better" depends on your tolerance for unhandled edge cases.

Integration breadth: Make wins on pure numbers

Make has roughly 3,000 native app integrations. n8n has about 400 official nodes plus 600+ community-built ones. If you use a lot of established SaaS tools — HubSpot, Salesforce, Shopify, Airtable — Make has you covered out of the box.

But here's the thing: n8n's HTTP Request node can connect to any REST API or GraphQL endpoint. If you're comfortable writing an API call, n8n can technically integrate with anything Make can, plus anything Make hasn't built yet. It's just more work upfront.

For marketing teams automating social posts and email sequences, Make's pre-built connectors save hours. For developers building custom integrations with internal tools or niche APIs, n8n's flexibility wins.

Self-hosting: the deciding factor for many

You can't self-host Make. Period. All your data, credentials, and workflow logic live on their infrastructure. They're SOC 2 compliant and GDPR compliant, which is fine for most use cases. But if you're in healthcare, finance, government, or just paranoid about vendor lock-in, this is a hard stop.

n8n runs anywhere Docker runs. A $6/month VPS from providers like Hostinger handles most small-team workloads. The Docker command is literally one line:

docker run -it --rm --name n8n -p 5678:5678 n8nio/n8n

Self-hosting means you control the database, the network, the uptime, and the encryption. It also means you're responsible for updates, backups, and monitoring. I set mine up with a weekly backup cron job and uptime monitoring in about 20 minutes. The trade-off is real but manageable.

Who should use what

Use Make if...

  • You're a marketing, sales, or ops team that needs automations running today
  • You use mainstream SaaS tools and value pre-built connectors
  • Nobody on your team wants to manage servers
  • Your volume is predictable and moderate (under 50,000 operations/month)
  • You want the visual builder that's easiest to learn from scratch

Use n8n if...

  • You need data to stay on your own infrastructure
  • You're building AI agents that need to make real decisions
  • You're running high-volume automations where per-operation costs add up fast
  • You have at least one person comfortable with Docker and basic devops
  • You need custom JavaScript or Python logic inside your workflows

What I actually kept after a month

I ended up keeping both. Make handles my marketing automations — social media scheduling, email sequences, data entry — because the pre-built modules mean I can throw something together in 30 minutes. n8n handles everything involving AI reasoning: the support bot, the lead qualification pipeline, and a few internal devops workflows where I needed full control over the logic.

If I had to pick one and money wasn't a factor? Probably n8n, self-hosted. The long-term cost is lower, the AI capabilities are meaningfully ahead, and I sleep better knowing my data isn't sitting on someone else's servers. But that's coming from someone who's comfortable with the command line. Your mileage will vary.

The real answer — and I realize this is the boring answer — is that they solve different problems for different people. Don't let anyone on Reddit or YouTube convince you there's a universal "best." There isn't. There's just the one that fits your team, your volume, and your tolerance for infrastructure management.

Bottom line

n8n and Make are both excellent in 2026. Make is polished, user-friendly, and has more integrations. n8n is more powerful, more private, and the AI agent capabilities are genuinely impressive. The price gap widens at scale — n8n's flat per-workflow model crushes Make's per-operation model when you're running thousands of automations per month.

If someone asked me "which one should I learn in 2026?" I'd say: learn both. Start with Make to understand the concepts, then move to n8n when you hit Make's limits. The skills transfer well enough, and having both in your toolkit makes you dramatically more valuable.
