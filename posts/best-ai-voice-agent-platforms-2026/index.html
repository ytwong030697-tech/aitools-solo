ElevenLabs: The voice quality king

If you have ever heard an AI voice and thought "wait, that's synthetic?", there is a good chance it was ElevenLabs. Their text-to-speech engine is genuinely unsettling in how natural it sounds. Sub-100ms latency means the agent responds faster than most humans do on a phone call. In March 2026 they announced a partnership with IBM watsonx, which puts their voice stack inside IBM's enterprise contact center ecosystem. That is a big deal for any business already on IBM infrastructure.

ElevenLabs is a full-stack platform. They build the TTS, speech-to-text, and LLM integration layers themselves rather than stitching together third-party providers. The upside is tight quality control and the best voice quality I heard in testing. The downside is less flexibility if you want to swap out components.

They support over 70 languages with automatic detection, and you can clone a custom voice from 30 seconds of audio. For businesses operating across markets, the language coverage alone is a reason to pick them.

Pricing runs $0.08 to $0.24 per minute depending on your model tier. That is competitive at the low end, though it creeps up if you need the highest-fidelity voices. If you want to test it, try ElevenLabs here (affiliate link — I earn a commission if you upgrade, at no extra cost).

I called a dummy support line set up with ElevenLabs and the agent handled a complex "I need to change my delivery address and also check if there is a delay on my order" request without dropping context. That is harder than it sounds.

Vapi: The developer's Swiss Army knife

Vapi takes the opposite approach from ElevenLabs. Instead of building everything in-house, it acts as an orchestration layer. You pick your TTS provider, your speech-to-text engine, your LLM, and Vapi stitches them together with one API. It handles the telephony, call recording, and analytics.

This flexibility is why Vapi processes 62 million calls per month with a 99.99% uptime SLA. Serious engineering teams gravitate toward it because they are not locked into any single provider. If one TTS service has a latency spike, Vapi fails over automatically.

The tradeoff: your voice quality depends entirely on which providers you choose. ElevenLabs TTS through Vapi sounds great. Cheaper TTS options through Vapi sound like a slightly upgraded IVR system from 2018.

Cost runs about $0.20 to $0.30 per minute all-in (Vapi's $0.05/min orchestration fee plus underlying provider costs). Higher than ElevenLabs but you get multi-provider redundancy. If your team has engineering resources and you care about avoiding lock-in, Vapi is probably where you end up.

Retell AI: Built for industries where mistakes are expensive

Retell focuses on a specific problem: what happens when an AI voice agent gets it wrong in a regulated industry? Healthcare, insurance, legal — these are places where a hallucinated answer is not just embarrassing, it is a liability.

Retell's dialog flow builder lets you define exactly what the agent can and cannot say at every step of the conversation. It reduces hallucination risk by constraining the agent's response space. SOC 2 compliant and HIPAA-ready, which is table stakes for healthcare. If you need to log every word of every call and prove compliance to an auditor, Retell is designed for that.

The catch: custom enterprise pricing with no public rates, and the platform is less flexible than Vapi for teams that want to tinker. You are buying guardrails and governance, not a playground.

Bland AI: When you need to make a lot of calls and spend less money

Bland is the outbound specialist. Lead qualification, appointment booking, payment reminders, survey collection — the kind of calling that burns out human SDRs within three months. If you are running outbound at serious volume (think 50,000+ calls per month), Bland is usually 30 to 50 percent cheaper than Vapi or Retell.

The latency is higher, around 800ms. For outbound calls that is fine. Nobody expects sub-100ms responses from a cold call. The base plan starts at $299 to $499 per month plus per-minute usage, with volume discounts.

Bland is not the platform you pick for voice quality. It sounds functional and gets the job done. The real value is the campaign management layer: CSV uploads, call schedules, script branching, and auto-logging outcomes to your CRM. If your primary use case is making a lot of calls efficiently, Bland is the most cost-effective option.

Which one should you actually pick?

I know "it depends" is annoying, but these platforms genuinely serve different needs. Here is my honest take:

  • Pick ElevenLabs if voice quality or multilingual support matters. Customer-facing calls where sounding human is the whole point. Start a free ElevenLabs trial (affiliate link).
  • Pick Vapi if you have an engineering team and want to avoid vendor lock-in. The multi-provider model is genuinely better for teams that can manage it.
  • Pick Retell if you are in healthcare, insurance, legal, or finance. The compliance posture and dialog guardrails are worth the custom pricing.
  • Pick Bland if you are doing high-volume outbound and care about cost per call above everything else.

FAQ

How much does it actually cost to run an AI voice agent?

At 1,000 minutes per month with GPT-4o-mini: ElevenLabs runs about $80 to $240, Vapi around $200 to $300, Bland starts at $299/month base. Retell requires a custom quote. This is still a fraction of what a human agent costs, but the economics only make sense at scale. Five hundred minutes per month is probably not worth the setup effort.

Can AI voice agents handle multiple languages?

ElevenLabs handles 70+ languages with automatic detection. Vapi supports about 20 languages depending on which providers you connect. Retell and Bland are more English-focused. If you operate across markets, ElevenLabs is the clear winner here.

Are AI voice agents good enough to replace human agents?

For well-scoped tasks like appointment scheduling, order status checks, and lead qualification: yes, the gap between a good AI voice agent and a human on a script is narrow enough that most callers will not notice or care. For open-ended problem-solving or emotionally charged calls: not yet, and probably not for a while.

Do I need to be a developer to set one up?

Depends on the platform. Vapi and Retell are built for developers. Bland has a no-code campaign builder. ElevenLabs is a middle ground: you can get a basic agent running without code, but anything beyond that benefits from some technical comfort. Synthflow (not covered here) is worth looking at if you want a pure no-code experience.

The bottom line

Voice AI agents are not a gimmick anymore. The latency is below the threshold where calls feel robotic, the pricing is transparent enough to model, and the integration options cover most CRMs and telephony setups. What is still missing is the polished "it just works" experience that non-technical teams need. But if you are willing to spend a weekend setting things up, you can have an AI agent handling real phone calls by Monday.
