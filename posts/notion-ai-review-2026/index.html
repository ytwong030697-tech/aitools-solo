Notion AI Review 2026: I Used It for 30 Days — Worth the Upgrade?

May 27, 2026 · 9 min read · By Friday

Bottom line upfront: Notion AI is a $10/month add-on that puts an AI assistant directly inside your workspace. After 30 days of real use — meeting notes, project briefs, database queries — I'd say it's worth it for about half of Notion users. For the other half, you're better off with Claude running in a separate tab.

Why I even bothered testing this

I've been a Notion user since 2020. It's where my business lives — project specs, content calendars, meeting notes, the whole mess. When they launched Notion AI, I ignored it. $10/month on top of an already-paid subscription? Seemed like a cash grab.

Then I watched a coworker use it during a sprint planning session. She highlighted three paragraphs of meeting notes, hit a button, and Notion AI turned the chaos into a structured brief with action items. Took about 8 seconds. I'd been doing that manually for years.

That stung enough to make me try it.

What Notion AI actually does (not what the landing page says)

The marketing page makes it sound like magic. It's not. Here's what you actually get:

Inside any Notion page, you hit the AI button or type /ai and pick from a menu of actions. The main ones I used:

  • Summarize: Highlight text → get a bullet-point TL;DR. Works on meeting notes, long docs, research dumps.
  • Generate from scratch: Give it a prompt and it writes a draft — meeting agendas, project briefs, blog outlines.
  • Translate: Surprisingly good. I tested it on a Korean client document and the English version was cleaner than Google Translate.
  • Fix writing: Grammar cleanup, tone adjustment, length trimming. Think Grammarly but inside Notion.
  • Database AI: This is the sleeper feature. You can ask questions about your databases in plain English — "show me all tasks assigned to Sarah that are overdue" — and it builds the filter for you.

The AI also shows up in search. You can ask "what were the action items from last Tuesday's client call?" and it digs through your workspace. Creepy, but useful.

The features I used every day (and the ones I forgot existed)

Meeting notes → action items: actual time saver

This is the feature that hooked me. After every call, I dump rough notes into Notion (bullet points, half-formed thoughts, random numbers). Select → "Generate action items" → I get a clean list of tasks with owners and deadlines inferred from context.

It's not perfect. Sometimes it makes up a deadline because it thinks "next week" means 7 days when I meant "the next business week, which is 3 days from now, please don't assign things to Saturday." But it gets it right about 85% of the time, which is honestly better than my manual process was.

Database AI: the feature I didn't know I needed

Notion databases are powerful but the filter syntax is a pain. Typing "show all blog posts in review status assigned to me, sorted by due date" and having it just work felt like cheating. It handles compound filters, multi-select properties, and date ranges without needing to click through dropdowns.

If you run a content calendar or task tracker in Notion, this alone might justify the $10.

Writing assistant: decent, not remarkable

I used the AI writing features for drafting blog outlines and cleaning up messy first drafts. It's fine. Grammarly-level corrections, basic tone adjustments. But here's the thing — Claude is noticeably better at writing. Claude understands voice, nuance, and context in ways Notion AI doesn't.

Where Notion AI wins: convenience. The AI is right there in the document. No copy-pasting between tabs, no switching contexts. For quick fixes and short drafts, the friction reduction matters more than output quality.

Features I stopped using after week one

The "brainstorm ideas" button generates lists that all sound the same after a while. The "explain this" feature reads like a Wikipedia summary. And I genuinely forgot the "continue writing" button existed until I went back to write this review.

Notion AI vs ChatGPT vs Claude (when to use which)

After a month of having all three open in tabs, here's where each one actually gets used:

Notion AI — summarizing meeting notes, generating action items, querying databases, quick writing cleanup inside documents. It's a convenience tool. You pay for "no context switching."

Claude — serious writing, complex reasoning, code generation, document analysis. When I need something smart, I open Claude. When I need something fast inside Notion, I use Notion AI.

ChatGPT — quick questions, image generation (DALL-E), web browsing. It's the Swiss Army knife. Notion AI is a specialized screwdriver — great at its job, but don't ask it to weld.

Realistically, if I had to pick one AI subscription on a budget, I'd keep Claude Pro and skip Notion AI. But having both? That's the setup I've landed on — and I'm not canceling either.

What still sucks

I want to be fair here. Three things genuinely annoyed me:

1. No memory between sessions. Every time you prompt Notion AI, it's a blank slate. It doesn't remember your preferences, your writing style, or what you talked about 10 minutes ago. Claude remembers across conversations. Notion AI acts like it has amnesia.

2. The AI can't act on your databases — only query them. You can ask "which tasks are overdue?" but you can't say "mark all overdue tasks as high priority and assign them to me." For an AI that supposedly lives inside your workspace, this is a weird limitation.

3. Output quality varies wildly by prompt type. Summaries and action items are solid. Creative writing and brainstorming are mediocre. The model seems tuned for structured, business-y tasks and falls apart when you ask for anything with personality.

Also, the mobile experience with AI features is clunky. But that's more of a Notion mobile problem than an AI-specific one.

Who should pay for Notion AI

Get it if:

  • Notion is your primary workspace (4+ hours/day)
  • You manage projects with Notion databases and hate writing filters
  • You take messy meeting notes that need cleaning up
  • Your team shares a Notion workspace and needs consistent summaries

Skip it if:

  • You use Notion casually (notes, grocery lists, occasional docs)
  • You already pay for Claude Pro and don't mind copying text between tabs
  • Most of your AI use is creative writing or brainstorming
  • You're budget-conscious and can only swing one AI subscription

Final verdict

Notion AI in 2026 is a genuinely useful product that happens to be priced about $3-5 too high for what it delivers. If it were $5/month, I'd tell everyone to get it. At $10, it's a judgment call.

For me, the meeting-note-to-action-items pipeline saves about 25 minutes a day. Multiplied by 20 workdays, that's 8 hours a month. Is 8 hours worth $10? Obviously. But if you don't take a lot of meeting notes or manage complex databases in Notion, you won't see that kind of return.

The database AI feature is the real differentiator and the one thing that might get better over time. If Notion ever lets the AI write to databases (not just read them), that changes the whole value proposition. Until then, it's a solid convenience tool for heavy Notion users and a shrug for everyone else.

If you want to try it, Notion gives you a limited number of free AI responses on any plan so you can test the waters before paying. No credit card required for the trial.
